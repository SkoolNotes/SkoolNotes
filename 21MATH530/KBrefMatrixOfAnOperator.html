Axler5.22 matrix of an operator, \(\mathcal{M} (T)\) def

Suppose \(T \in \mathcal{L} (V)\) and \(v_1, \ldots, v_n\) is a basis of \(V\). The matrix of $T$ wrt this basis is the n-by-n matrix \[\begin{aligned} \mathcal{M} (T) = \begin{pmatrix}A_{1,1} & \cdots & A_{1, n} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ A_{n, 1} & \cdots & A_{n, n} \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}\] whose entries \(A_{j, k}\) are defined by \[\begin{aligned} Tv_k = A_{1, k}v_1 + \cdots + A_{n,k}v_n \end{aligned}\]

Specify a basis with \(\mathcal{M} \left( T, (v_1, \ldots, v_n) \right)\)

intuition

each column is where the map takes a basis vector

Simplifying The Matrix Representation

’A central goal of linear algebra is to show that given an operator \(T \in \mathcal{L} (V)\), there exists a basis of \(V\) wrt which \(T\) has a reasonably simple matrix’

If by simple we mean "has many zeros" or RREF, then we know enough to ensure that there exists a basis s.t. the first column has zeros everywhere except the first row.

\[\begin{aligned} \begin{pmatrix}\lambda &&&\\0&*&&\\\vdots&&&\\0&&&\end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}\] Where \(\) denotes all the other entries. Find \(\lambda\) by taking the lone eigenvalue and letting it’s eigenvector be the first basis vector.
